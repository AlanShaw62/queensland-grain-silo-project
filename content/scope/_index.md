\# Project Scope and Nomenclature



This project is a photographic survey of the grain handling facilities built or operated by the former \*\*Queensland State Wheat Board\*\* and its successors. 



\## Scope of the Project



The project includes:



\- Former Queensland State Wheat Board silos and bulkheads  

\- Grain receival sites operated by successor organisations  

\- Locations where structures no longer exist  

\- Supporting infrastructure such as weighbridges, out‑loading equipment and rail sidings (where possible)



The operational dates of the silos are from the early 1950s, up until about 1992, although in a couple of locations I have included later facilities built by GrainCorp. 



My starting point was a Queensland State Wheat Board map that I found in the Queensland State Archives. This 1981 map, titled Location Map of State Wheat Board Depots, was my key to planning trips to photograph whatever might be there, although of course I learned from further research that the Board did construct further facilities after this map was produced.



The accessibility of these facilities varies, and as a result so too does my photographic coverage. Some sites are still in use by GrainCorp, so photos of these facilities tend to be over the fence from the boundary. Likewise those facilities those facilities tat have been sold to individuals or private companies have been photographed from the boundary line. I chose to document more closely the remaining facilities where I could, exercising care, common sense and judgment about where I may have wandered with my camera.





The project does \*\*not\*\* include:

* the original bagged wheat sheds of the Queensland State Wheat Board, most of which have long since been removed, although if one still exists at a facility otherwise in scope I may have included a photo or two
* Private farm silos  
* Non‑grain agricultural storage



\## Terminology Used



\*\*Silo\*\*  

A vertical concrete or steel storage structure used for bulk grain. Unlike for example New South Wales, as far as I can determine Queensland did not have a particularly sophisticated classification system beyond being a basic description. In Queensland, the broad categories typically found in Queensland State Wheat Board documents were:



* squat silos - the first bulk storage facilities built in the early 1950s, these were low circular structures, made principally from concrete 
* Haunstrup silos - the next generation of bulk storage built in the mid to late 1950s, these were tall concrete silos named for one of the early construction contractors
* Munro silos - also built from the late 1950s to the mid 1960s, these were also tall concrete silos, named for a consulting engineer who was subsequently appointed to the Queensland State Wheat Board
* SA Concreate silos - these silos appeared through the 1960s to the early 1970s, and were also tall concrete silos so named because they were based on the design of silos used in South Australia
* Concrete and steel silos - generally a smaller silo built through the mid-1960s to the early 1970s, this design had silos of concrete for approximately a quarter of their height, with the remainder being steel
* Concrete silos - the final major design, these had a more slender and taller look than the earlier designs and appeared from the mid 1970s through the 1980s



There were some further types, much less prevalent than those listed above:

* Profit Center Concrete Silos - a slimmer design, vary rarely used around 1980. I don't know why they have that name
* Steel (Lipp) Silos - rarely used in the early 1980s, they have a quite different corrugated steel look. Lipp is a major European silo supplier still in operation today.



\*\*Bulkhead\*\*  

A horizontal bunker‑style storage facility, with two main types in Queensland:

* the Perrin shed - a long low shed built throughout the 1960s and into the early 1970s, named for a senior officer of the Queensland State Wheat Board
* R\&N Horizontal Storage - a more substantial looking shed, less widespread than the Perrin shed, built by R\&N Trading Co, who are still in business trading as RanSheds



\*\*Receival Site\*\*  

A location where grain was delivered, weighed, graded and stored.



\*\*Out‑loading\*\*  

The process of transferring stored grain to rail or road transport.



\*\*Status Categories\*\*



\- \*\*Active\*\* — still in use for grain handling  

\- \*\*Standing (inactive)\*\* — structure remains but no longer used  

\- \*\*Removed\*\* — structure demolished or no longer present  



\## Geographic Boundaries



The project covers all the former Wheat Board sites across Queensland, including:



\- Darling Downs  

\- South‑West Queensland  

\- Central Queensland  

\- Burnett and Wide Bay regions  



\## Sources

A disappointing early discovery in this project was that a lot of historic material and documentation from the Board doesn't appear to have survived. The Queensland State Archives do have the hand written Resolution Books from each Board meeting, but does not seem to have Minute books or the papers for each meeting, nor does it have documentation from the various committees of the Board. Oddly, I have yet to find Annual Reports of the Board for much of the period of this project's scope.



The John Oxley Library in the State Library of Queensland does have some records from the late 1980s to the early 1990s, and in particular the Don McKechnie Papers.



The Board commissioned a history - Going With the Grain - A History of Queensland's State Wheat Board by RS Marriott and G McLean.



All these sources have been useful, but even so I have yet to find for example a definitive source that shows typical facility plans, silo designs and drawings or even when a silo was completed. I may yet find this information, but the impression I have is these records have possibly been destroyed as the Board went through its various iterations before being sold to GrainCorp.



\## Dates

For each facility, I have attempted to show when the Board first approved a contract for its construction. The Board's Resolution Books do show the Final Payments for some of the contracts, which can be implied to be close to when the facility became available for use. I originally set out to use these dates to indicate when the facility was first open for use, but encountered two problems: the first was that not all contracts had the information in the Resolution Book, and the second was that some contracts covered several separate sites. In the latter case, the Final Payment may be as long as five years after the contract was let, so applying a date to an individual site without more information seemed to be potentially misleading. 



As a result, I opted for the approval date from the Resolution Book, on the basis that I can be confident about that date, although it does not overcome the problem that where a contract covered several sites, construction at some of those sites in the contract may have been  years after the initial approval.
